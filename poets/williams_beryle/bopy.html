The Boy Next Door

for J. J.  

 

He brings me letters

with words so strange

I’m sure they must be written

in some ancient script,

flowers before

they’ve begun to bloom,

feathers from birds

I’ve never seen.

 

Much is mystery

to him

and to me when he’s here.

 

Today he is

flapping around on my porch

bumping the walls

with something.

Finally he reaches

my door, knocks.

 

“Hi,” he says, his face

more sober than usual.

“Today I’m being a bird.”

Two stick-hung U.S.

flags have become

his wings, another

nondescript plastic one

his tail. “I can tell,”

I reply, “I never thought

of using flags like that.

Do they work pretty well?”

 

His wings have become

crossed.  Working hard,

he is finally rearranged

and gasps “Yes, they do.

Do you....” (now his tail

has slipped and he twists

to get it back in

the proper place.) “Do you...”

he begins again,

“ever want to be a bird?”

 

“Oh, often,” I reply,

“but first I have to learn

how to fly!” I can’t even read

his look but without

another word except for

one last swipe of a drooping

tail on my dusty porch

he flies off, leaving me

stranded.

No flags.

 

(reprinted with thanks from THEMA: Lost in Translation, THEMA Literary Society, Summer, 2003)
