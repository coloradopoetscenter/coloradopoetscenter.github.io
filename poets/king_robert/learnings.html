Learnings

           “I’ll teach you,” my mother said,
“to iron your shirt--” raising the board
           with its creaking spring, then lock,
her iron of scorch and shine, my shirt
           lying with delicate open arms--
“So you won’t have to marry for that.”
           And I have ironed my long life, married,
unmarried, loving the hiss of pressure,
           my own shape unwrinkled into slick edges.
Another time, she said she didn’t want
           a wife to write my family letters
and I have also written my self away,
           in love with duty and the hot weight
of syllables, dressing news spiffily up.
           Even now I love the shining field
of the back, the problems of arms,
           pressing a crisp attention to the words
flattened each day in letters to my father,
           inventing enough for two lives.

(reprinted with thanks from Shenandoah, Winter, 2001)
