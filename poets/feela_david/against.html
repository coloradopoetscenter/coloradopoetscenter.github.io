Against the Piedra

Before I left home for the Piedra River I packed

a dozen nonessential things, like a winter hat,

a dictionary, two pounds of roofing nails

 

and a ping-pong paddle. It was blazing summer

and I needed a feeling of luxury, of being

on my own with everything to lose.

 

I parked the car behind a convenience store

where the highway touches a forest

service road, and followed a trail sign

 

dividing a crooked path. One way climbed

then flattened above the river, sixteen miles

with a bird’s-eye view of the shining worm below;

 

the other way tumbled like a stone to the river’s edge,

scrambled over river rock and through every root-tangle

where the cottonwoods crowded together.

 

I must have carried sixty pounds, food

enough for a monastery, my foolishness

strapped to my back, and I began descending

 

like water tipped from a pitcher, like Dante

refreshed by a cool draft on his way

through a strange wood. I met not a soul

 

except the possibility of my own, then found myself

standing toe to toe with a hundred feet of the Piedra.

If this were hell it would be bone cold,

 

white spittle boiling over the bank, the river

twisting itself into a cable, as if to pull

an enormous boulder that glistened like the cusp

 

of a tooth. My shoulders ached, my heart sounded

a drum from some deeper place

where I had yet to descend.

 

–Mountain Anthology, forthcoming Spring, 2005
