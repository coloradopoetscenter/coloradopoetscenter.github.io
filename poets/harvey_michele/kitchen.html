I Am Not In My Mother’s Kitchen

Aunt Beth climbs the stairs to the third floor.
At the end of a dark hall she fumbles
with the deadbolt.

She walks through a narrow foyer
takes a sharp right, and enters the kitchen
belonging to my mother.

The kitchen has homely green curtains,
An old wood pantry, and a freezer which like mother
needs defrosting.

The railroad rumbles a glass of old scotch
on the wooden counter, and water’s boiling
on the old gas stove.

Beth has shown up to shop for lamps
with mother, but discovers her slumped
on the white vinyl floor.

There’s no sign of forced entry, though few came
to see mother willingly.  And now Beth is afraid
to touch her.  Even alive, my mother was untouchable.

Beth turns off the stove, calls the police.
She reaches for the scotch, curses God for being left
to deal with this mess.

There will be no new lamp to liven this empty space.
There is no blood.
There is no sorrow.

Beth curses the police,
who like mother, failed to arrive,
when needed.

(Wazee Journal)
