Poems: Waiting the Diagnosis | Attempt | So I Keep On Reckoning | Picking Up a Hitchhiker in May

May Day, 2004

“Memory—what is it? And can I get to it?” –Gregory McGuire

 

Before you know what memory is,

you must stop moving, preferably

finding a seat in the sunlight outdoors,

perhaps on a squared railroad tie,

its brown grain weathered to white.

Does the tie remember when it was a tree,

what it felt like to reach for sun,

to surrender its leaves to drought?

You can’t know this, only suppose.

All you can know is the pile of pulled dandelions

wilting on the gray sidewalk beside you;

one of the flowers has gone to seed,

its unspent white wishes nestled

into the bed of green stems and forked leaves.

Does the dandelion recall when it was golden

and plump? What orange song would it sing?

But this is imagination, the twin sister of memory,

the longing to know what we are unable to see.

It is the hunger for tastes no longer in our mouths.

In January, it is the ache for the green bittersweet

of asparagus; in October for the bulbous red celebration

of raspberries fresh from the side of the road;

when the lover has gone, it is the thirst for the salt of his neck.

Before you know memory, you must see

how imagination travels everywhere with her.

As soon as you walk away from the iris leaves,

you wonder, were they really like fat green tongues

licking up toward the alder, or were they slender spears?

Were there buds just about to bloom? And come next week,

you may not recall that you saw the iris leaves at all.
