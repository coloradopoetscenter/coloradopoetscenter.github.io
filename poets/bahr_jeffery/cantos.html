Jeffery Bahr

One stanza written with a three-penny

on the hidden side of sheet rock – six weeks

stint in a Chelsea development, then shipped out

on a long shot, lines out to haddock,

taking leave from the hood of his sou’wester, tacked

on to the last of his stanzas (gilled last

gasps), rap of the Atlantic

monthly over the gunwales, past the tops

of his galoshes, take and double take. Time

for an outer bridge, polished slope to

the dull interior, this new republic

blank blue, his mouth a monoplane.

Making plans (portside a prairie,

schooner in his lee, lines left

cockeyed on the bulkheads), something

involving the cross-connect of used brick

and the fricatives of Tampa, Nebraska.

Reviews his progress: She’s still

dead and he’s the clutch plate. Sulfur fringe,

birds gorging in the black eye – north

to the third coast, south to the levees, west

to how many mountains, moving against

such clouds or is it the clouds moving? Here’s some

verse for their spindrift: Stand from the fosse,

leave me my bloody bever. He appropriates

half-built models in the bedrooms of the children

(stock cars, fighter planes) and the long-hauler

yells for a heavy piece for another tier, and he sleeps

beneath packing blankets, someone’s squared-up possessions

headed for the borderlands, annotations

black on boxboard: Second Bedroom,

Medicine Cabinet. They rattle

in the throat of the road. They Morse

as she would have diddled lyrics

into jay-squawk. He the meat and she

the nerve, and what now, running

out of nation, only the desert

left. And the deep blue that the desert

dreams. And rocks that need rearranging.
